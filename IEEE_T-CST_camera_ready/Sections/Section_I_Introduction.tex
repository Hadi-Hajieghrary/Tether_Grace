Cooperative aerial transport uses $N$ multirotor uncrewed aerial vehicles (UAVs) to lift a common payload through flexible cables. The platform has been demonstrated in laboratory and outdoor experiments for disaster-response resupply, suspended construction, and heavy-payload scenarios~\cite{Michael2011, Fink2011, Bernard2011, Tagliabue2019}. We focus on the regime in which fault response sets the platform's limiting behavior. Cable severance may stem from abrasion, attachment-hardware fatigue, or obstacle contact; the severed cable's tension redistributes across the remaining cables within one payload-pendulum period, and if the controller cannot absorb this redistribution within that window the payload drops. Unlike actuator or sensor faults in single-vehicle systems, cable severance is a structural change in the plant: a discrete reduction of the constraint set, not an additive perturbation. It arrives without supervisory signal and requires a corrective response on the payload-pendulum timescale, typically well under one second.

Three structural features of this plant—unilateral compliant coupling~\cite{Brogliato2016}, hybrid constraint-cardinality transitions at severance events~\cite{GoebelSanfeliceTeel2012}, and strict information locality (each vehicle observes only its own state, rope tension, and a local payload estimate)—rule out classical cooperative and slung-load architectures. Any admissible controller must deliver post-fault response without detection, annunciation, or supervisory reconfiguration, and must admit a closed-loop analysis that remains valid across the structural transitions.

This paper shows that, for this plant class, fault tolerance is delivered by a single feed-forward identity in the per-drone controller rather than by detection and reconfiguration: drone $i$'s altitude thrust feed-forward is set equal to its own measured rope tension, $T_i^{\mathrm{ff}}(t) = T_i(t)$ (formally \eqref{eq:Tff} in \cref{sec:proposed}).

Here $T_i^{\mathrm{ff}}$ is the thrust feed-forward added to drone $i$'s commanded acceleration, and $T_i$ is its measured rope tension. The rationale is timescale-based: when a peer's cable severs, the load redistributes across the remaining cables within one payload-pendulum period ($\approx 2$~s), and the surviving drone's measured tension rises during that window. Identity \eqref{eq:Tff} converts that rise at the next control tick into a proportional thrust increment, absorbing the new load at the plant's native rate without detection or communication. The mechanism sits inside a conventional cascade---an outer-loop proportional-derivative (PD) controller with anti-swing slot shift, a single-step convex quadratic program (QP) on the tilt-and-thrust envelope, and a geometric attitude loop. Identity \eqref{eq:Tff} is the sole nonstandard component.

We develop the consequences of \eqref{eq:Tff} along three axes. The first contribution is a taut-cable reduction theorem (C1, \cref{sec:reduction-and-reference}): within a slack-excursion-bounded domain parameterized by the pendulum-to-rope timescale ratio $\delta$ and a bound $\eta$ on cable-slack duty cycle, an explicit first-order error budget justifies replacing the rope's distributed spring-damper dynamics with a lumped effective stiffness. The admissibility gates (slack-run duration $\le \SI{40}{\milli\second}$, duty cycle $\le 2.5\%$, QP active-set-transition fraction $\le 1\%$) are met by all six demonstrated missions with margin; worst observed values are $\SI{35.8}{\milli\second}$, $1.66\%$, $0.10\%$ (\cref{tab:domain-audit}). The second contribution is a baseline hybrid practical input-to-state stability (ISS) theorem (C2, \cref{sec:stability}): on the C1 domain, under a single-active-regime doctrine for the QP active set and a spaced-fault dwell hypothesis $\tau_d \ge \tau_{\mathrm{pend}}$, the closed-loop tracking error admits a closed-form recovery envelope with Lyapunov decay rate $\lambda$ and per-fault-cycle contraction $\rho \in (0,1)$. The strict $\rho < 1$ is achieved by identity \eqref{eq:Tff}, which certifies Lyapunov contraction across each fault cycle rather than mere non-divergence within a pendulum period. The dwell hypothesis is structural, not conservative: it is the minimum time over which the measured-tension feed-forward $T_i^{\mathrm{ff}} = T_i$ can restore the Lyapunov function below its pre-fault level with $\rho < 1$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:dwell-decay}). The third contribution is a closed-form stability bound for the $L_1$-adaptive altitude augmentation (C3, \cref{sec:extensions}): for the discrete-time $L_1$ outer loop on the altitude channel, a certifiable gain-selection condition is given in closed form in the control-tick period and the altitude-channel entry of the outer-loop Lyapunov matrix, removing the dominant hyper-parameter of $L_1$ from empirical tuning.

The proposed controller is validated in high-fidelity Drake multibody simulation through a pre-declared six-variant capability demonstration and four targeted stress campaigns on a five-drone, \SI{10}{\kilo\gram}-payload, \SI{4}{\metre\per\second} Dryden-wind scenario under single and compound cable-severance faults. The feed-forward ablation campaign isolates identity \eqref{eq:Tff} as the causal mechanism: disabling it materially inflates cruise tracking error and post-fault altitude sag (\cref{tab:ff-ablation}). Two null findings are reported alongside: in these regimes, a composable extension layer is structurally inactive because the baseline already satisfies the relevant constraint. The architecture admits a sequence of $F \le N - 2$ unannounced severances under the actuator-margin envelope (\cref{sec:fault-and-controller}); the demonstrated $N=5$, $m_L=\SI{10}{\kilo\gram}$ admits $F \le 2$.

The contribution is best read as a category claim---about how to handle structural transitions whose information arrives through actuator-side measurements on the plant's native timescale---rather than as a feature combination on a five-drone case study. Four architectural consequences follow from \eqref{eq:Tff}: \emph{locality} (the admissible information set $\mathcal{I}_i$ \eqref{eq:info-set} contains no peer state, no fault flag, no shared scheduler), \emph{passivity} (the same identity that operates pre-fault produces the post-fault response, with no detector, classifier, or reassignment rule), \emph{robustness to $F \le N - 2$ unannounced severances} by induction on the fault counter under the actuator-margin envelope $m_L g/(N{-}F) \le \kappa_{\mathrm{act}} f_{\max}$ \eqref{eq:actuator-envelope}, and \emph{team-size independence} (the per-drone law and the lemma stack of \cref{sec:stability} carry no $N$ structurally; the dependence concentrates in the actuator-margin envelope). These properties are audited against the campaign in \cref{sec:discussion-advance}; the two null findings (\cref{rem:mpc-null,rem:reshape-null}) are read here as evidence that the conventional active fault-tolerance (FT) machinery is not what is doing the work.

Section~\ref{sec:Problem_Statement} formalizes the plant, fault model, admissible controller class, reference trajectory, taut-cable reduction theorem, and the formal problem. Section~\ref{sec:proposed} presents the decentralized controller and its three-layer cascade. Section~\ref{sec:stability} establishes the main hybrid practical-ISS theorem through five supporting lemmas and characterizes its robustness. Section~\ref{sec:extensions} adds the $L_1$ altitude augmentation, receding-horizon MPC with penalized tension-ceiling constraints, and the post-fault formation-reshape supervisor, and shows each preserves the baseline theorem's recovery envelope on its admissibility domain.

\subsection{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

Related work falls into four categories: centralized and distributed cooperative transport, geometric tracking and slung-load control, $L_1$ adaptive control, and fault-tolerant control. We map these and identify the gap this paper addresses.

\paragraph{Centralized and distributed cooperative transport.} Force-allocation architectures originating with Michael, Fink, and Kumar~\cite{Michael2011} solve a centralized QP over cable tensions to satisfy wrench-closure and actuator-limit constraints; subsequent refinements improve robustness and payload-mass estimation~\cite{Fink2011, Tagliabue2019} but preserve synchronous global state. Distributed predictive control~\cite{WangOng2017} replaces per-tick global communication with iterative neighbor exchanges, but message complexity still scales with horizon and coupling rank. Recent work extends nonlinear MPC to six-degree-of-freedom rigid-body payload transport~\cite{LiLoianno2023}, adds control-barrier inter-vehicle safety~\cite{PallarLi2025}, and handles parametric payload uncertainty across directed communication topologies~\cite{SuBhowmick2023}. At agile multirotor tick rates, the per-tick synchronous global-state requirement common to all of these formulations is a structural barrier to operation at rates where cable severance can occur within a single outer-loop horizon. None of these architectures accounts for cable severance in its stability argument; each assumes a fixed constraint set over the control horizon.

\paragraph{Geometric tracking and slung-load control.} The foundational single-quadrotor geometric tracking controller~\cite{LeeLeokMcClamroch2010}, the flatness-based slung-load framework~\cite{Sreenath2013}, and the $\mathrm{SE}(3)$ geometric control for a rigid-body suspended payload~\cite{Lee2018} all sit under a fixed-constraint-set assumption. Multi-vehicle extensions~\cite{LeeSreenathKumar2013, SharmaSundaram2023} reintroduce rigid-formation kinematics or global-state exchange, both of which collapse the decentralization that motivates the multi-vehicle architecture.

\paragraph{$L_1$ adaptive control.} $L_1$ adaptive control~\cite{CaoHovakimyan2008, Hovakimyan2010book} delivers matched-disturbance rejection with transient-performance bounds, recently extended to the full-$\mathrm{SE}(3)$ dynamics of a single quadrotor~\cite{WuCheng2025}; the extension to cooperative architectures under cable-fault events and strict locality remains open.

The fault-tolerant control literature divides into active architectures that reconfigure after diagnosis~\cite{Blanke2015, ZhangJiang2008} and passive architectures that absorb faults through robustness~\cite{Patton1997}. Within cooperative aerial transport, two prior strands are directly relevant. Fault-tolerant cooperative transportation under suspension-cable failures has been addressed through observer-based disturbance estimation with dynamic load reassignment~\cite{LiuSuspensionFailure2021}: each surviving vehicle estimates a tension-related disturbance, and the load allocation is redistributed by a reassignment law. Communication-free or low-communication cooperative transport is achieved by passive force control~\cite{GassnerPassiveForce2017} and leader–follower architectures with force estimation~\cite{NoCommTransport2021}, which avoid inter-UAV message exchange but presuppose a fixed cable-cardinality regime. These strands show that neither cable-severance response nor strict locality is independently novel here. The present contribution is best read not as a feature subtraction but as a different commitment about \emph{how the structural-transition announcement is processed}: the actuator-side tension measurement \emph{is} the announcement, eliminating the detection-classification-reconfiguration timeline as a system-level construct rather than substituting a faster version of it. The architectural category occupied by this paper is decentralized cooperative manipulation in which discrete structural transitions self-announce through actuator-side measurements; the cable-severance instance is the certification vehicle, not the contribution.
