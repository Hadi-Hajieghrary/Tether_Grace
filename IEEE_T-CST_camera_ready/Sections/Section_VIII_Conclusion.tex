This paper showed that a simple decentralized feed-forward identity enables passive fault tolerance to abrupt cable severance in cooperative aerial transport. By setting each drone’s altitude thrust feed-forward equal to its measured cable tension, the controller converts load redistribution into immediate control action using only local measurements. This mechanism removes the need for fault detection, communication, or reconfiguration and operates on the timescale of the plant dynamics.

We analyzed the resulting closed loop as a hybrid system and established a practical input-to-state stability result under structural fault transitions. The analysis produced an explicit recovery envelope with exponential decay and per-fault-cycle contraction and guaranteed bounded tracking error under actuator and dwell-time conditions. A reduced-order model supported the analysis and bounded the effect of cable dynamics on the closed loop.

High-fidelity multibody simulations demonstrated that the controller absorbs single and multiple cable severance events within one payload-pendulum period. Feed-forward ablations support the interpretation that the identity is the active recovery mechanism within the tested baseline architecture and showed that additional control layers remained inactive on the demonstrated trajectory family.

These results suggest that, on the stated admissibility domain, passive local control laws can certify recovery for self-announcing cable-severance transitions. Future work will extend the analysis beyond the slack-excursion-bounded domain, investigate hardware validation, and explore applications to other multi-agent and contact-rich systems.
