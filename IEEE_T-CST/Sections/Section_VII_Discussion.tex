This section interprets the results, identifies the mechanism and its limits, situates the findings relative to prior work, and outlines what remains open.

\paragraph*{The central finding and its mechanism}
The full-Drake results (Tables~\ref{tab:scenario_summary}--\ref{tab:matched_single_fault}) confirm that the GPAC-based architecture absorbs one to three abrupt cable-snap faults without explicit fault detection or reconfiguration, with RMSE decreasing monotonically with team size. Relative to the Tether\_Lift baseline~\cite{hajieghrary2026tetherlift}, this is Tether\_Grace's central novelty: the same N-agnostic local-sensing architecture that enabled decentralized cooperative lift also absorbs topology changes. Proposition~\ref{thm:topology_invariance} explains this structurally---topology changes affect only the disturbance input, not the system matrices---and the tension feedforward ablation (Table~\ref{tab:tension_ablation}) pinpoints the mechanism: PID feedback combined with gravity feedforward, not cable-tension feedforward ($<\!1\%$ RMSE contribution). Gravity feedforward provides the baseline hover thrust requiring no knowledge of other agents, PID feedback corrects the tracking-error displacement, and the integral term compensates the increased load share (subject to the anti-windup saturation discussed in Section~\ref{Section_IV:Fault-Tolerant_Control_Architecture}). That cable-tension feedforward contributes so little was itself counter-intuitive; the authors initially hypothesized it as the primary mechanism.

\paragraph*{Why the result is non-trivial}
While PID with integral action rejects bounded step disturbances in single-input linear systems, the extension to multi-agent systems with elastic cable coupling, underactuation, and unobservable topology changes is not obvious. The gap between the scalar-model prediction (${\sim}18$~cm) and full-plant observation (${\sim}107$~cm)---a factor $\alpha \approx 5.8$---quantifies the aggregate contribution of effects the scalar model omits: elastic cable coupling (the bead-chain produces force oscillations the scalar model treats as smooth), attitude-loop delay (${\sim}37\%$ bandwidth overlap between the 5~ms inner-loop period and the ${\sim}8$~ms force-transmission timescale), and multi-axis interaction through $R_i$. The small-gain analysis confirms that a proper multivariable treatment is needed to close this gap formally, while the simulation evidence confirms qualitative consistency with the scalar prediction. Additional secondary effects operate within the fault-tolerant envelope without altering the primary mechanism:
%
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Anti-swing damping} ($k_{\mathrm{sw}} = 0.5$) is small compared to PID forces during the fast cable-snap impulse but attenuates the slower ($1$--$2$~s) post-fault pendular oscillation between trajectory direction changes.
  \item \emph{Cable stiffness} $k_s$ affects transient duration and wave speed ($v_{\mathrm{wave}} \propto \sqrt{k_s}$) but not impulse magnitude, which is set by $T_j \approx m_L g / N$. Higher stiffness produces a sharper but not larger transient; the finite-time bound~\eqref{eq:tracking_bound} accommodates this variation through $\tau_{\mathrm{fault}}$.
  \item \emph{Freed-agent escape} was revised during evaluation after the original parameters required ${\sim}60$\textdegree\ tilt (below-gravity vertical thrust). The revised two-waypoint design is conservative but reliable; a systematic parametric study falls outside this paper's scope.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph*{Design tools and their validation}
The $f_{\max}$ boundary sweep (Table~\ref{tab:fmax_boundary}) validates the thrust-margin screen by quantifying the exact failure boundary at ${\sim}45$~N, coinciding with the theoretical minimum~\eqref{eq:fmax_critical} with the default $f_{\max} = 150$~N providing $3\times$ headroom. This yields an actionable design criterion: $f_{\max} > (N m_Q + m_L)g/(N{-}k) + f_{\mathrm{track}}$. The matched single-fault data (Table~\ref{tab:matched_single_fault}) further shows that peak error is nearly constant across team sizes (${\sim}107$--$112$~cm) while RMSE improves because larger teams recover faster during mid-trajectory tracking. Together with the closed-form cost ratio $\rho_{\mathrm{FT}} = N/(N{-}k)$ and the calibrated transient prediction, these tools provide a complete actuator-sizing methodology for fault-tolerant cooperative transport.

\paragraph*{From hypothesis to structural property}
Proposition~\ref{thm:topology_invariance} identifies topology-independent error dynamics for a simplified model, and four lines of empirical evidence support qualitative extension to the full plant: thrust margins ($1.75$--$2.33$ capacity ratios), monotonically decreasing RMSE with~$N$, a quantified thrust boundary at the theoretical minimum, and fault-count isolation at $N{=}7$. Three formal gaps remain (Section~\ref{sec:topology_invariance_theorem}): impulsive slack-to-taut forces, attitude-loop delay under fast transients, and the cable-force small-gain condition. Closing these---particularly the $\mathcal{H}_\infty$-based small-gain verification via Drake's \texttt{Linearize()}---remains the primary theoretical open problem.

\paragraph*{Comparison with prior work}
Liang~et~al.~\cite{liang2021fault} address suspension failure via centralized reconfiguration with known failure identity; the present work shows neither assumption is necessary, achieving stable tracking ($44$--$49$~cm RMSE) without failure identification or communication. Zeng~et~al.~\cite{zeng2025decentralized} observed implicit resilience in MARL-based cable transport but did not explain the mechanism; Proposition~\ref{thm:topology_invariance} provides the explanation and predicts that \emph{any} N-agnostic architecture with adequate thrust margin should behave similarly---the topology invariance is a structural property of the design, not specific to PID control. The centralized oracle (Table~\ref{tab:baseline_comparison}) achieves comparable performance ($+0.7\%$), demonstrating that local cable sensing already captures nearly all fault-relevant information.

\paragraph*{Remaining open problems}
Five directions remain for future work:
%
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Adaptive residual integration.} An $\mathcal{L}_1$ adaptive controller~\cite{cao2010l1book,cao2010stability} is implemented in the codebase. Its key property for topology invariance is that $\hat{d}$ carries no pre-fault history (unlike CL's buffer of $M{=}50$ pairs). Head-to-head comparison with PID-only under ESKF+wind conditions is planned as near-term future work.
  \item \emph{Integral gain sensitivity.} The integral time constant $1/k_i \approx 6.7$~s sets the post-fault recovery timescale and determines $\rho_{\mathrm{FT}}$. A sweep of $k_i$ would characterize the tradeoff between faster recovery and transient overshoot, complementing the $f_{\max}$ boundary sweep.
  \item \emph{Formal extension to the full plant.} Three self-contained technical problems remain: bounding impulsive slack-to-taut forces, analyzing cascaded position/attitude stability under fast transients, and verifying the small-gain condition for cable-force feedback.
  \item \emph{Cable pendulum management.} The figure-eight trajectory excites near-resonance (${\sim}2$~s cable pendulum period vs.\ ${\sim}3$~s corner interval). Trajectory shaping with pendulum frequency avoidance may be needed for sustained aggressive maneuvering.
  \item \emph{Hardware validation.} All results are simulation-based. Physical multi-quadrotor cable transport with actual cable-snap events remains the ultimate test.
\end{itemize}
