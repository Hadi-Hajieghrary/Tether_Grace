% ======================== ABSTRACT ==============================
\begin{abstract}
When a cable snaps during multi-quadrotor cooperative payload transport, the payload drops, the freed agent accelerates away, and every surviving agent faces an instantaneous load-share jump---a~$50\%$ increase for $N{=}3$. This paper shows that a decentralized, communication-free architecture absorbs such faults with no detection, no reconfiguration, and no inter-agent communication.

The mechanism is a structural property of N-agnostic GPAC (Geometric Position and Attitude Control): because each agent's PID-plus-gravity-feedforward law uses only local measurements with fixed gains, the per-agent error dynamics share the same system matrices regardless of cable topology (Proposition~1). Cable snaps change the disturbance input but not the system. This topology invariance yields two closed-form design tools: a fault-tolerance cost ratio $\rho_{\mathrm{FT}} = N/(N{-}k)$ for actuator sizing and a finite-time transient bound whose scaling is confirmed to be topology-independent across scenarios. Ablation identifies PID feedback with gravity feedforward---not cable-tension feedforward ($<\!1\%$ RMSE contribution)---as the primary fault-absorption mechanism.

Full-Drake multibody simulations across $N \in \{3, 5, 7\}$ with up to three cascading cable snaps demonstrate payload RMSE of $43.6$--$48.8$\,cm, \emph{decreasing} with team size despite absorbing proportionally more faults. A centralized oracle with perfect fault knowledge improves over the passive architecture by only~$0.7\%$, showing that the price of decentralization under cable faults is effectively zero. Results are validated under sensor-realistic conditions (ESKF~+~Dryden wind), Monte Carlo campaigns ($30$~runs, $<\!3\%$ RMSE variation), and a thrust-saturation boundary sweep identifying a sharp phase transition at the theoretical minimum with~$3\times$ headroom.

The topology-invariant error structure is proven for a simplified per-agent model. Extension to the full nonlinear bead-chain plant is supported by simulation evidence and the ISS switched-system framework; the formal stability proof remains an open problem.
\end{abstract}
